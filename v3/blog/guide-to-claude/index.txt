20:T19d4,<p>Hey — I&#39;m Parker. I help small business owners in the trades, real estate, and local services actually use AI in their day-to-day work. Not tutorials. Not demos. Real tasks, real results, starting week one.</p>
<p>Most of my clients have heard of ChatGPT. Fewer have heard of Claude. That&#39;s a problem, because for a lot of the things small business owners actually need — writing, summarizing, analyzing, staying organized — Claude is genuinely better.</p>
<p>This guide is for you if you&#39;ve never opened Claude before or barely touched it. I&#39;ll walk you through what it is, how to set it up, and five prompts you can use today.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Claude vs. ChatGPT: what&#39;s actually different</h2>
<p>Both are AI assistants. Both can write, answer questions, and help you think through problems. The difference is in the feel — and in a few specific strengths that matter for business owners.</p>
<p>Claude tends to write in a more natural, nuanced way. It&#39;s less likely to give you corporate-speak. When I ask it to write an email in my voice, it actually sounds like me after just a little coaching. ChatGPT is fantastic and I use it too, but its default writing style is more &quot;AI assistant&quot; and less &quot;person.&quot;</p>
<p>Claude is also very good at reading long documents. You can paste in a 10-page contract, a lease agreement, a competitor&#39;s pricing page — and ask it questions. That capability is a game-changer for anyone who deals with a lot of paperwork.</p>
<p><strong>ChatGPT</strong></p>
<h3>Strengths</h3>
<ul>
<li>Huge ecosystem of plugins and GPTs</li>
<li>Image generation (DALL-E)</li>
<li>More widely known, lots of tutorials</li>
<li>Strong at coding tasks</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Claude</strong></p>
<h3>Strengths</h3>
<ul>
<li>More natural writing voice</li>
<li>Reads and analyzes long documents</li>
<li>Better at nuanced, judgment-heavy tasks</li>
<li>Projects feature for persistent context</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2>How to set up a free account</h2>
<p>Go to claude.ai. Click &quot;Sign up.&quot; Use your Google account or your email. That&#39;s it — you&#39;re in. The free tier gives you access to a capable model with daily message limits. It&#39;s plenty to get started and figure out if it&#39;s worth upgrading.</p>
<p>If you use it heavily and hit the daily limit, the paid plan is $20/month and gives you access to Claude&#39;s most powerful model (Sonnet and Opus) with much higher limits. For most business owners, the free tier is fine for the first couple weeks while you&#39;re learning.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Don&#39;t overthink the setup. Create the account, open a new chat, and type something. The fastest way to understand what Claude can do is to just start asking it things.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
<h2>Claude Projects: your secret weapon</h2>
<p>One of the most underrated features in Claude is Projects. A Project is a persistent conversation space where you can give Claude background information about your business that it remembers every time you come back.</p>
<p>I help clients set up a Project called &quot;My Business&quot; where they paste in: what they do, who their typical customers are, what their tone of voice sounds like, and any recurring context they always have to re-explain. From then on, every conversation in that Project starts with Claude already knowing the basics.</p>
<p>Instead of saying &quot;I&#39;m a 12-person landscaping company in Edina, we focus on residential clients, our tone is friendly and professional&quot; every single time, you say it once. Claude remembers it. That alone saves several minutes every session and makes every output more relevant.</p>
<hr>
<h2>5 starter prompts that work out of the box</h2>
<p>Here are five prompts you can use today. Copy them, swap in your details, and hit send.</p>
<p><strong>1. Email follow-up:</strong> &quot;I&#39;m a [type of business] owner. Write a short, friendly follow-up email to a customer who received a quote three days ago and hasn&#39;t responded. Keep it under 100 words. Don&#39;t be pushy.&quot;</p>
<p><strong>2. Social media post:</strong> &quot;Write an Instagram caption for a [type of business]. The topic is [specific thing that happened this week or a tip you want to share]. Conversational tone, end with a call to action. Under 80 words.&quot;</p>
<p><strong>3. Summarize a document:</strong> &quot;Here&#39;s a [contract / lease / proposal / article]. Please give me a plain-English summary of the key points, any red flags, and the three things I need to pay most attention to.&quot; Then paste the document.</p>
<p><strong>4. Meeting prep:</strong> &quot;I have a meeting tomorrow with a potential client who [describe what they need]. Help me prepare: what questions should I ask, what should I know going in, and what objections should I be ready for?&quot;</p>
<p><strong>5. Rewrite in your voice:</strong> &quot;Here&#39;s a paragraph I wrote: [paste your text]. Rewrite it to sound cleaner and more professional, but keep my casual tone. Don&#39;t make it stiff.&quot;</p>
<blockquote>
<p>These prompts aren&#39;t magic formulas. They&#39;re starting points. Run one, see what comes back, then say &quot;make it shorter&quot; or &quot;change the tone&quot; or &quot;add a line about our guarantee.&quot; Two rounds of iteration beats one perfect prompt every time.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
<h2>What Claude is better at than anything else</h2>
<p>If I had to pick one thing Claude does better than any other tool I&#39;ve tried, it&#39;s this: reading something messy and turning it into something clear. Long emails, confusing contracts, rambling notes from a job site — give it the raw material and ask it to make sense of it. It does this extraordinarily well.</p>
<p>The second thing is maintaining a consistent tone across a lot of writing. Once it understands your voice (which you can teach it in a Project), it can draft emails, social posts, and proposals that all sound like they came from the same person. That coherence is hard to achieve manually when you&#39;re running a business and writing things at 6am before a job and 9pm after one.</p>
<p>You don&#39;t need a tech background for any of this. You just need to be willing to try, iterate, and give it good context. That&#39;s the whole game.</p>
