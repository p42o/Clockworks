<hr>
<h2>The truck-cab problem</h2>
<p>The job (fixing the furnace, framing the deck, snaking the line, replacing the panel) is the easy part. You&#39;re trained for it. You&#39;re paid for it. You enjoy it.</p>
<p>The hard part is everything around the job. The follow-up texts. The &quot;did you get the estimate?&quot; check-ins. The review requests. The 11pm Facebook message asking &quot;do you serve Eden Prairie?&quot; The appointment confirmations. The &quot;I&#39;m 15 minutes out&quot; texts. The annual maintenance reminders. The invoicing nudges.</p>
<p>This is the boring 30%. And it&#39;s killing your business. Not because you&#39;re bad at it, but because you can&#39;t be bad at it AND under a sink at the same time. So you do it after hours. Or on Saturday. Or you don&#39;t do it at all.</p>
<p>The trade businesses crushing it in 2026 figured something out: that boring 30% is the cheapest, easiest, highest-ROI thing in your operation to hand to a system. Not because the system is smart. Because the system is <em>consistent</em>. And you, with the best intentions in the world, cannot be consistent when you&#39;re 12 feet up a ladder.</p>
<hr>
<h2>The five automations every Twin Cities trade should have running</h2>
<h3>1. Missed-call text-back</h3>
<p>When a customer calls and you can&#39;t pick up, an automated text fires within five seconds: <em>&quot;Hey, sorry I missed you. What can I help with?&quot;</em></p>
<p>Four out of five callers reply by text. Without it, they hang up and call the next name on Google.</p>
<p>Setup: 15 minutes. Cost: free with most CRMs. The single highest-ROI automation we build, period.</p>
<h3>2. The 3-text estimate follow-up</h3>
<p>Most estimates die on a counter or in an inbox, not because the customer hates the price but because life moves on. The fix: a sequence that politely keeps the conversation alive without being pushy.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>+24 hours:</strong> &quot;Just checking if you had any questions about the estimate.&quot;</li>
<li><strong>+72 hours:</strong> &quot;Still interested? I&#39;ve got availability opening next week.&quot;</li>
<li><strong>+7 days:</strong> &quot;Last check-in. No pressure, just wanted to make sure you&#39;re taken care of.&quot;</li>
</ul>
<p>Reply at any point and the sequence stops. A Bloomington contractor we work with took his close rate from 18% to 31% in the first month. Same leads, same prices, same crew. The only thing that changed was that nobody got forgotten.</p>
<h3>3. The review automation</h3>
<p>One hour after a job is marked complete, an automated text goes out: <em>&quot;Thanks for having us out. Everything looking good?&quot;</em> If yes, the next message includes a direct link to your Google Business Profile. If anything below 3 stars, you get an alert before the review goes public, so you can call the customer and make it right.</p>
<p>A Wayzata roofing crew went from 14 reviews in 18 months to 27 new 5-star reviews in 30 days. They were already earning the reviews. They were just never asking for them at the right moment.</p>
<h3>4. Two-text appointment confirmations</h3>
<p>The single cheapest way to cut your no-show rate by ~70%:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>T-24 hours:</strong> &quot;We&#39;ll see you tomorrow at [time]. Reply C to confirm.&quot;</li>
<li><strong>T-2 hours:</strong> &quot;On our way in about 2 hours.&quot;</li>
</ul>
<p>An Eden Prairie HVAC company set this up last fall. No-show rate dropped from 22% to 6% in the first month. They eliminated roughly four wasted truck rolls a week, and at an internal cost of about $110 each, that&#39;s $1,760 a month back in their pocket. From two text messages.</p>
<h3>5. Past-customer rebooking</h3>
<p>The automation almost no one is running, and the one that prints money: every twelve months after a service, an automated message fires to the past customer asking if they&#39;d like to schedule the same service again. With a direct booking link.</p>
<p>This is free revenue from people who already trust you. It runs forever after one setup. For a landscaping business in Plymouth, this single automation booked 38% of their spring cleanup work last year, work that used to take their office manager three weeks of cold calls every February.</p>

<h2>Why &quot;I&#39;ll just do it myself&quot; is the most expensive sentence in trades</h2>
<blockquote>
<p>If you bill at $60 an hour and you spend ten hours a week on follow-ups, review requests, appointment confirmations, and answering &quot;do you serve Plymouth?&quot;, then you&#39;re using $600 a week of your most valuable resource on tasks that a system could handle for less than $5.
<strong>That&#39;s $31,200 a year. To send texts and answer the same four questions over and over.</strong></p>
</blockquote>
<p>And that&#39;s just the dollars. The real cost is what the admin work does to your evenings, your weekends, the dinners you missed because you were quoting an emergency call at 7pm.</p>
<p>You wouldn&#39;t hand-dig a foundation when you own an excavator. Stop hand-sending follow-up texts when a system could do it while you sleep.</p>
