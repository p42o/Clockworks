25:T1966,<p>Hey, I&#39;m Parker. I run Clockworks, and part of what I do is help people stop paying for AI tools they&#39;re not getting real value from. It&#39;s more common than you&#39;d think.</p>
<p>There&#39;s a pattern I see over and over: someone tries a cheap AI tool, gets mediocre output, decides &quot;AI isn&#39;t ready yet,&quot; and misses out on the tools that actually could help them. The problem wasn&#39;t AI. The problem was the wrong tool at the wrong price point.</p>
<p>Here&#39;s what separates cheap AI from AI that sticks, and what I actually recommend at each budget level.</p>
<hr>
<h2>Why $5/month tools mostly disappoint</h2>
<p>The AI tools in the $5–$15/month range are almost always built on older model versions with strict output limits, fewer customization options, and less context capacity. They can handle short, simple tasks. But the moment you give them something nuanced (a situation with history, a task requiring judgment, a document longer than a page), they fall apart.</p>
<p>The other issue is that cheap tools rarely let you customize for your specific context. You can&#39;t teach them your voice, your business, your typical customer. Every session starts from scratch. So the output always feels generic, because it is. The model doesn&#39;t know you&#39;re an HVAC company in Minnesota. It just knows whatever you type in the first two sentences of your prompt.</p>
<p>I don&#39;t want to say all budget tools are worthless. Grammarly at $12/month is excellent for what it does. But anything that promises to write your marketing copy, draft your proposals, and handle your customer emails for $7/month is usually making promises it can&#39;t deliver.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The cheapest option isn&#39;t the free tier of a bad tool. It&#39;s the free tier of a good tool. Claude and ChatGPT both have free tiers that outperform most paid cheap tools. Start there before you spend anything.</p>
</blockquote>
<hr>
<h2>What separates tools that stick</h2>
<p>In my experience, the AI tools that people actually use six months after buying them have a few things in common.</p>
<p>First, they fit into the workflow that already exists. The tools that fail are the ones that require you to go somewhere new, learn a new interface, and change how you already work. The tools that stick are the ones that show up where you already are (in your email, your notes, your documents) or that become a simple, memorable habit (like opening Claude every morning).</p>
<p>Second, they have enough context capacity to handle real work. Real work involves long emails, full documents, complex situations with multiple parties. If a tool tops out at 500 words of context, it can&#39;t help you with the tasks that take the most time.</p>
<p>Third, they allow you to teach them your preferences. The best tools let you set persistent instructions (your tone, your business context, what to avoid) so you&#39;re not recalibrating every single session.</p>
<hr>
<h2>The 3 questions to ask before buying any AI tool</h2>
<p>Before you subscribe to anything, ask these three questions:</p>
<p><strong>1. What specific task am I hoping this handles?</strong> Not &quot;make me more efficient.&quot; Be specific. &quot;Write follow-up emails&quot; or &quot;summarize meeting notes&quot; or &quot;draft social media content.&quot; If you can&#39;t name the task, you can&#39;t evaluate whether the tool actually does it well.</p>
<p><strong>2. Can I test it on that task for free before paying?</strong> Almost every legitimate AI tool has a free tier or a trial. If a tool asks for your credit card before you&#39;ve seen it handle your actual use case, walk away. Use the free version to run your specific task and see what comes back.</p>
<p><strong>3. Will I actually open this thing every day?</strong> This one is underrated. The best AI tool is the one you actually use. If it requires 10 steps to get a result, you&#39;ll stop using it by week two. If it&#39;s genuinely fast and simple for your use case, you&#39;ll build a habit. Habit is where the time savings actually compound.</p>
<p><strong>What Cheap AI Looks Like</strong></p>
<h3>Red Flags</h3>
<ul>
<li>Promises everything, specializes in nothing</li>
<li>No persistent context or memory</li>
<li>Limited output length on key tasks</li>
<li>Requires you to start from scratch every session</li>
<li>Generic output that doesn&#39;t sound like you</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What Good AI Looks Like</strong></p>
<h3>Green Flags</h3>
<ul>
<li>Handles long, nuanced inputs well</li>
<li>Lets you set persistent instructions</li>
<li>Free tier is genuinely useful</li>
<li>Output improves with iteration</li>
<li>Fits naturally into how you already work</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2>What Parker actually recommends at each budget level</h2>
<p><strong>Budget: $0/month</strong></p>
<h3>Start with Claude or ChatGPT free tiers</h3>
<p>Both are genuinely excellent. Claude&#39;s free tier is my first recommendation for business writing tasks. ChatGPT&#39;s free tier is better if you want integrations or are doing anything technical. Use one of these for 30 days before you spend a dollar. You&#39;ll either get enough value from the free tier to keep going, or you&#39;ll hit the limits and know it&#39;s worth upgrading.</p>
<p><strong>Budget: $20–$25/month</strong></p>
<h3>Claude Pro or ChatGPT Plus</h3>
<p>Once you&#39;re hitting daily limits on the free tier, this is the right upgrade. Both are $20/month. Claude Pro gets you more messages, the full Claude Sonnet model, and extended Projects capability. ChatGPT Plus gets you GPT-4o with higher limits and access to more integrations. Pick the one you&#39;ve been using and upgrade it. Don&#39;t switch tools, just upgrade.</p>
<p><strong>Budget: $50+/month</strong></p>
<h3>Microsoft Copilot (if you&#39;re in 365) or a focused specialist tool</h3>
<p>At this budget level, you&#39;re either running Microsoft 365 and Copilot makes sense as an integrated add-on, or you have a very specific use case (like Perplexity Pro for heavy research). I don&#39;t recommend this tier until you&#39;ve been consistently using a $20/month tool for at least 60 days. Don&#39;t jump here early. Most small businesses don&#39;t need to be here at all.</p>
