Why Your ChatGPT Prompts Aren't Working
(And the Simple Fix)

Vague questions get vague answers. Here's the formula that changes everything.

Parker Swanson
CEO, Clockworks  ·  Apr 6, 2026  ·  6 min read

Hey — I'm Parker. I run Clockworks, an AI consulting firm in Maple Grove, MN. Most of my clients come to me after the same frustrating experience: they tried ChatGPT, got garbage back, and concluded that "AI just isn't for me."

Nine times out of ten, the problem isn't the tool. The problem is the prompt. And the fix is simpler than you think.

This post is going to show you exactly why vague prompts fail, the three-part formula I teach every client on day one, and three real before/after examples you can steal right now.

Why vague prompts fail

ChatGPT is not a search engine. When you type "help me with my email," it has almost nothing to work with. Who's the email to? What's the situation? What do you want the person to do after reading it? What tone are you going for?

Without that context, the model defaults to the most average, generic response possible. It's not being lazy — it's doing exactly what you asked. You gave it almost no information, so it gave you almost no value back.

Think of it like hiring a contractor. If you say "fix my house," they're going to stare at you. If you say "replace the kitchen faucet, it's leaking from the base, and I need it done by Friday because company's coming" — now they can actually help you.

The AI isn't the problem. The instructions you're giving it are. A better prompt takes 30 extra seconds to write and gets you 10x better output.

The formula: Context + Task + Format

I teach every client a simple three-part formula. Once you see it, you'll use it automatically. It goes: Context (who you are and the situation), Task (exactly what you need done), and Format (how you want the output delivered).

Context is where most people skip. Tell the AI who you are, who you're talking to, and what the background is. "I'm the owner of a 10-person HVAC company in Minnesota" gives it so much more to work with than nothing at all.

Task is the actual ask. Be specific. Not "write an email" but "write a follow-up email to a customer who got a quote last Tuesday and hasn't responded yet."

Format tells it how you want the output. "Keep it under 150 words, friendly but professional, no bullet points" will save you from getting a five-paragraph essay when you needed three sentences.

Three before/after examples

Let's make this concrete. Here are three real prompts I've seen clients use, and what they look like after applying the formula.

Before

Email Prompt

  • Write an email to a customer
  • Result: generic, stiff, unusable
After

Email Prompt

  • I'm an HVAC owner in MN. Write a 100-word follow-up to a customer who got a quote on Tuesday and hasn't responded. Friendly, not pushy. End with a question.
  • Result: send-ready in 60 seconds
Before

Social Post Prompt

  • Write something for Instagram about my business
  • Result: vague fluff, no personality
After

Social Post Prompt

  • I run a plumbing company. Write an Instagram caption about why people should fix small leaks before they become big ones. Conversational, a little humor, end with a CTA to call us. Under 80 words.
  • Result: actually sounds human
Before

Proposal Prompt

  • Help me write a proposal
  • Result: a generic template with fake numbers
After

Proposal Prompt

  • I'm a landscaper writing a proposal for a $4,200 backyard renovation. The client is a homeowner in her 40s who cares about low maintenance and privacy. Draft a one-page proposal: overview, scope, timeline, price, and a short why-us section.
  • Result: 90% ready to send

The "iterate" mindset

Here's the shift that separates people who get real value from AI and people who give up after one try: they treat it like a conversation, not a one-shot request.

Your first prompt doesn't have to be perfect. Send it, see what comes back, then say "make it shorter" or "make it sound less formal" or "add a line about our warranty." You're not failing when you refine — that's how it's supposed to work.

I had a client — a property manager in Plymouth — who spent 20 minutes trying to craft the perfect prompt before hitting send. I watched her do it and finally said, "Just send whatever you have. We'll fix it in two replies." She got exactly what she needed in under four minutes total. The iterating was faster than the over-thinking.

Think of ChatGPT as a very capable assistant who needs good directions. You wouldn't hand a new employee a sticky note and expect a finished report. Give them context, give them the task, tell them how you want it delivered — and then course-correct as needed.

Once you internalize Context + Task + Format and stop expecting perfection on the first try, you'll be shocked how much faster your workday gets. I've watched people go from "AI doesn't work for me" to "I can't believe I was doing this by hand" in a single afternoon session.

That's the real unlock. Not a new tool, not a subscription upgrade. Just a better way of asking.
