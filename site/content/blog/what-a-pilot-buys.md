Every price on my site is public: the audit is free, the pilot is $2,000 to $5,000 fixed, the managed service is $99 to $299 a month. What the site can't show you is what those two pilot weeks actually feel like from your side of the counter. So here it is, day by day.

To be clear up front: this isn't a story about a client. It's the standard process — what a typical build week looks like for anyone who signs on. Yours will differ in the details; it shouldn't differ in the shape.

## Day 0: a number, not a range

By Day 0 we've already done the free 30-minute audit, and you're holding a one-page plan that says which system matters most for your shop — usually one of Call Catch, Quote Chaser, Review Engine, or Paper Mover, sometimes two.

The quote I hand you is a fixed number. Not "starting at." Not "depending on complexity." A number, in writing, that doesn't move.

Why fixed? Because hourly software work invites the same disease you've seen in your own trade from the other side — the change order. "We got in there and found more than we expected" is sometimes true behind a shower surround. In software it's usually a sign somebody didn't scope the job before quoting it. The scoping is my risk, not yours. If I misjudge how gnarly your phone setup is, I eat the hours. You'd extend your customers the same courtesy on a bid; I'd be embarrassed not to extend it to you.

## Hour 48: something you can poke

Two days after you say go, you get a demo. Not slides. Not a video of somebody else's system. Your greeting, your business name, and a text that lands on your actual phone when you call your actual number and let it ring out.

The 48-hour demo exists for one reason: nobody should pay for promises they can't poke. Software people love to sell futures — roadmaps, mockups, "phase two." You wouldn't pay a plumber who described a water heater real nice. So before the pilot is three days old, there's a live thing you can call, text, break, and argue with. If it's wrong, we find out at hour 48, not week six.

Fair warning: the demo is rough. The wording is my first draft of your voice, and my first draft of your voice is wrong in ways only you can hear. That's the point of the next ten days.

## Week 1: the shadow run

Now the system runs alongside your real life — real calls, real customers — but with training wheels on. Every message it sends, you see. Every summary lands in your pocket. And we tune, usually over text, usually in five-minute bursts, because you're on a job site and I know it.

This week is mostly about words. "We'd never say 'reach out' — we say 'give us a shout.'" "Don't text customers after 8." "Mrs. Hendricks on the maintenance plan gets a phone call, not a sequence." Ten small corrections like that are the difference between a system that sounds like your shop and one that sounds like a bank. This is also the week we find the weird stuff — the second phone line nobody mentioned, the tech who marks jobs complete two days late — while a human is still watching every message.

## Week 2: the handover

The last week is about making me unnecessary. You get the passwords — all of them, in your password manager, not mine. You get a plain-English document, usually two or three pages: what runs, where it lives, what it costs monthly in raw tool fees, how to change the wording, and — this is the one people underline — how to turn it off. Every system I build has an off switch you can reach without calling me.

Then it goes live for real, and you choose what's next. Most owners take the managed service — $99 to $299 a month, and I watch it, tune it, and answer when something's weird. No contract; any month you can stop, and everything keeps running under your accounts because it was built there from Day 0. Or you take the keys and run it yourself. Either answer is fine with me. A client who stays because leaving is painful isn't a client — that's a hostage, and it's a lousy business model for a guy whose name is on the thing.
