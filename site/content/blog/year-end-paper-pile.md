Somewhere in your truck right now there's a door pocket with receipts in it. Somewhere in your books there's an invoice from July with an open balance, and another from September, and you're not mad about them exactly — you just haven't had a minute, because it's the busy season, and the busy season is precisely when the paper pile grows fastest.

Here's the November problem in one sentence: every unpaid invoice and unfiled record you carry into January costs more there than it does here. Your accountant will ask questions you'll answer worse from memory. And an invoice that felt awkward to chase at 60 days feels borderline hopeless at 150 — not because the customer turned into a deadbeat, but because everyone involved is now embarrassed.

## The polite-nudge math

Let me do this with made-up numbers so we can see the shape of it. Say you've got $14,000 in open invoices spread across 11 jobs. Odds are one or two of those customers have genuinely decided not to pay, and no email will fix that. The rest are people who meant to pay and lost the thread — the invoice went to a spam folder, a spouse thought the other one handled it, life moved.

For that second group, the fix costs almost nothing: a short, friendly reminder with the amount and a payment link. No guilt, no lecture. Most of what comes back is some flavor of "oh shoot, sorry — paying now." The nudge isn't collections. It's a courtesy, and most customers receive it as one. The only reason it doesn't happen is that sending it requires you to sit down, look at the aging report, feel a little awkward, and type — eleven times — during your busiest month. So it doesn't happen, and $14,000 of finished work ages like the truck receipts.

## Close the year clean: the DIY sequence

Here's a sequence you can run yourself, no software from me required. One pass a week, four weeks, done before the holidays eat the calendar.

**Week 1 — see the whole pile.** Pull the open-invoice report from QuickBooks (or Jobber, or Housecall Pro — they all have one), oldest first. Add the stragglers you never entered. You can't chase what isn't written down, and half the relief of this whole exercise is just seeing the real number.

**Week 2 — first nudge, everything over 30 days.** One template, personalized lightly: "Hi [name] — tidying up the books before year-end and noticed invoice #214 from the water heater job is still open. Here's the link if it slipped by. Thanks again for having us out." Year-end is the perfect excuse; it makes the nudge about your bookkeeping, not their character.

**Week 3 — second nudge, plus a phone call for anything past 90.** The second message can name a date: "hoping to close the books by December 19." Past 90 days, text stops working and a two-minute phone call starts. Decide now what you'll write off, and write it off cleanly — a small known loss in December beats a mystery in March.

**Week 4 — records for the accountant.** Reconcile the bank feed. Photograph the door-pocket receipts into one folder. Export the year's job list. Your accountant's January email has maybe six questions in it every year; this week is you answering them in advance, while you still remember what that $340 at the supply house was for.

That's it. An evening a week for a month, and you walk into January clean.
