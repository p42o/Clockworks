## Claude vs. ChatGPT: what's actually different

Both are AI assistants. Both can write, answer questions, and help you think through problems. The difference is in the feel — and in a few specific strengths that matter for business owners.

Claude tends to write in a more natural, nuanced way. It's less likely to give you corporate-speak. When I ask it to write an email in my voice, it actually sounds like me after just a little coaching. ChatGPT is fantastic and I use it too, but its default writing style is more "AI assistant" and less "person."

Claude is also very good at reading long documents. You can paste in a 10-page contract, a lease agreement, a competitor's pricing page — and ask it questions. That capability is a game-changer for anyone who deals with a lot of paperwork.

## How to set up a free account

Go to claude.ai. Click "Sign up." Use your Google account or your email. That's it — you're in. The free tier gives you access to a capable model with daily message limits. It's plenty to get started and figure out if it's worth upgrading.

If you use it heavily and hit the daily limit, the paid plan is $20/month and gives you access to Claude's most powerful model (Sonnet and Opus) with much higher limits. For most business owners, the free tier is fine for the first couple weeks while you're learning.

> Don't overthink the setup. Create the account, open a new chat, and type something. The fastest way to understand what Claude can do is to just start asking it things.

## Claude Projects: your secret weapon

One of the most underrated features in Claude is Projects. A Project is a persistent conversation space where you can give Claude background information about your business that it remembers every time you come back.

I help clients set up a Project called "My Business" where they paste in: what they do, who their typical customers are, what their tone of voice sounds like, and any recurring context they always have to re-explain. From then on, every conversation in that Project starts with Claude already knowing the basics.

Instead of saying "I'm a 12-person landscaping company in Edina, we focus on residential clients, our tone is friendly and professional" every single time, you say it once. Claude remembers it. That alone saves several minutes every session and makes every output more relevant.

## 5 starter prompts that work out of the box

Here are five prompts you can use today. Copy them, swap in your details, and hit send.

**1. Email follow-up:** "I'm a [type of business] owner. Write a short, friendly follow-up email to a customer who received a quote three days ago and hasn't responded. Keep it under 100 words. Don't be pushy."

**2. Social media post:** "Write an Instagram caption for a [type of business]. The topic is [specific thing that happened this week or a tip you want to share]. Conversational tone, end with a call to action. Under 80 words."

**3. Summarize a document:** "Here's a [contract / lease / proposal / article]. Please give me a plain-English summary of the key points, any red flags, and the three things I need to pay most attention to." Then paste the document.

**4. Meeting prep:** "I have a meeting tomorrow with a potential client who [describe what they need]. Help me prepare: what questions should I ask, what should I know going in, and what objections should I be ready for?"

**5. Rewrite in your voice:** "Here's a paragraph I wrote: [paste your text]. Rewrite it to sound cleaner and more professional, but keep my casual tone. Don't make it stiff."

> These prompts aren't magic formulas. They're starting points. Run one, see what comes back, then say "make it shorter" or "change the tone" or "add a line about our guarantee." Two rounds of iteration beats one perfect prompt every time.

## What Claude is better at than anything else

If I had to pick one thing Claude does better than any other tool I've tried, it's this: reading something messy and turning it into something clear. Long emails, confusing contracts, rambling notes from a job site — give it the raw material and ask it to make sense of it. It does this extraordinarily well.

The second thing is maintaining a consistent tone across a lot of writing. Once it understands your voice (which you can teach it in a Project), it can draft emails, social posts, and proposals that all sound like they came from the same person. That coherence is hard to achieve manually when you're running a business and writing things at 6am before a job and 9pm after one.

You don't need a tech background for any of this. You just need to be willing to try, iterate, and give it good context. That's the whole game.
