Hey — I'm Parker. I test AI tools constantly as part of my consulting work, but I've never written up my honest ranking in one place. So here it is. No sponsorships, no affiliate links, no softened opinions.

I ran the same set of tasks on all eight tools: write a follow-up email, summarize a one-page document, draft a social media post, answer a question about a specific industry (HVAC), and respond to a vague, poorly-written prompt. The goal was to see how each tool handled real small business use cases — not fancy demos.

---

## The rankings

### 1. Claude (Anthropic) — Top Pick

Claude is my go-to for anything involving writing or reading long documents. The output quality is consistently high, the tone is natural, and it's the best tool I've found for making AI sound like an actual human wrote it. The Projects feature — where you can give it persistent context about your business — is genuinely useful and something I set up for every client.

It handles vague prompts better than almost anything else. When I gave it a messy, unclear ask, it asked one clarifying question instead of just guessing wrong. That alone saves a lot of time.

Best for: Business writing, email, proposals, document summarization, anything where tone matters.

### 2. ChatGPT (OpenAI) — Top Pick

ChatGPT is excellent and for many people it's the right primary tool. The ecosystem is unmatched — there are more integrations, plugins, and community-built resources for ChatGPT than everything else combined. If you're technical and want to connect it to your other tools, this is where you start.

The default writing voice is a little more "AI assistant" than Claude — polished but slightly generic. Fixable with good prompting, but worth noting. The free tier is generous and GPT-4o is a genuinely capable model at every tier.

Best for: General use, technical tasks, automation integration, coding help.

### 3. Perplexity — Solid

Perplexity is a research tool, not a writing tool. It's the AI I use when I need information with actual citations — sourced, up-to-date, with links I can verify. For competitive research, market information, or industry questions, it's better than asking Claude or ChatGPT because it shows you where it got the information.

Don't use it to write emails. Use it to answer questions. That's what it's for, and it does it well.

Best for: Research, fact-checking, competitive intelligence, staying current on your industry.

### 4. Microsoft Copilot — Solid

If your business runs on Microsoft 365 — Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams — Copilot is worth the add-on cost. It's genuinely useful right inside the tools you already use. Drafting emails in Outlook, summarizing meeting notes in Teams, building formulas in Excel — it integrates in a way the other tools can't match.

As a standalone tool? It's decent but not a top pick. Its value is entirely contextual: if you're a Microsoft shop, it punches above its weight. If you're not, skip it.

Best for: Microsoft 365 users who want AI in their existing workflow.

### 5. Notion AI — Niche

Same story as Copilot: it's a contextual tool. If you already use Notion for your notes, project management, or documentation, Notion AI is a natural fit. It can summarize pages, help you draft inside your notes, and fill out templates. It's not trying to be a general-purpose AI.

If you don't use Notion, there's no reason to start just to get Notion AI. And if you do use Notion, you'll find the AI useful but limited compared to Claude for actual writing quality.

Best for: Notion users who want AI inside their workspace.

### 6. Grammarly — Niche

Grammarly is a writing assistant, not an AI assistant. It's excellent at what it does — catching errors, improving clarity, checking tone — and the browser extension is genuinely useful when you're writing emails or documents across the web. The AI-generated suggestions have improved a lot.

But I wouldn't call it an AI tool in the same category as the others. It's more like a very smart editor. It won't draft a proposal for you from scratch. Use it alongside one of the top-ranked tools if clean, polished output matters to you.

Best for: Catching errors and polishing drafts you've already written.

### 7. Gemini (Google) — Skippable

I wanted to rank Gemini higher, and Google has been improving it. But in my testing, it consistently felt a step behind Claude and ChatGPT for the tasks small business owners care about. The writing output was more generic. It struggled more with nuanced prompts. And the integration into Google Workspace, while improving, isn't as seamless as Copilot in Microsoft.

If you're all-in on Google Workspace and want something embedded there, it's worth trying. Otherwise, start with the top two first.

Best for: Google Workspace users who want to stay in the Google ecosystem.

### 8. Jasper — Skip It

Jasper costs $49+/month and is built for marketing teams at larger companies. It produces decent marketing copy, but ChatGPT and Claude do it just as well for $20/month or free. In my testing, I couldn't find a single use case where Jasper justified its price premium over the alternatives.

For small business owners, it's not the right tool. The price is high, the output isn't better enough to justify it, and the learning curve means you'll spend time on setup that you could spend actually working.

Best for: Enterprise marketing teams with dedicated content staff. Not for most small businesses.

## My recommendation by use case

**For Writing**

### Best Tool

- Claude for emails, proposals, social
- ChatGPT as a strong alternative
- Grammarly to polish the final draft

**For Research**

### Best Tool

- Perplexity for cited, current info
- Claude or ChatGPT for analysis
- Gemini if you're Google-first

**For Microsoft Users**

### Best Tool

- Copilot inside your existing apps
- Claude for standalone writing tasks

**Starting from Zero**

### Best Tool

- Claude — free tier, easy to learn
- One tool, get good at it, then expand

> The worst thing you can do is subscribe to six tools and use none of them consistently. Pick one, use it every day for a month, and get good at it. Then decide if you need a second. Most small business owners don't need more than two.
