Last month I wrote about why your AI gives you generic answers: it doesn’t know you. And I introduced the idea of a **Context Vault** — one organized document that holds everything an AI should know about your business so it can stop guessing.

That post got the most replies of anything I’ve published. About half of them were some version of:

> “Okay, this makes sense. But what actually *goes* in a Context Vault?”

Fair question. Here’s the structure I use — the same one I build for every client. Seven sections. Each one is there because, when it’s missing, your AI’s answers fall apart in a specific, predictable way.

---

## The seven sections

### 01. Who you are

The basics: business name, your name, location, years in business, legal structure. **Why it matters:** without this, your AI will draft you a Mailchimp template every time. With it, the AI knows you’re a one-person shop in Maple Grove, not a 50-person agency in San Francisco.

### 02. What you sell

Your services or products, your pricing, what each one includes and excludes, what’s upsell vs. core, what you refuse to sell. **Why it matters:** the AI can’t draft a quote, write a service page, or answer a customer question if it doesn’t know what you actually offer. Most people forget the “what you refuse to sell” part — that’s often the most useful detail.

### 03. Who buys from you

Your customer types, what their day looks like, what they actually care about, what objections they raise, where they find you. **Why it matters:** good marketing copy is specific. If your AI doesn’t know your customer, it writes “business owners who want to scale” for the tenth time and it’s meaningless.

### 04. How you sound

Voice and tone. Words you say, words you refuse to say. A few sample paragraphs in your real voice that the AI can pattern off. **Why it matters:** this is the single biggest lever for “does the AI sound like me or like a robot.” A two-paragraph voice description with examples will outperform any prompting trick on the planet.

### 05. How you actually work

Your process. How a customer goes from first contact to paying invoice. What you do, what your team does, what gets outsourced, what gets dropped. The boring operational truth. **Why it matters:** the AI can’t help you fix bottlenecks or draft follow-up messages if it doesn’t know what comes next in your process.

### 06. The tools you live in

Your stack. Gmail or Outlook. Google Calendar or iCal. Which CRM, which scheduler, which docs app, which accounting tool, what’s on your phone vs. your desktop. **Why it matters:** this is what makes the “do things, not just talk” part work. When your AI knows your stack, it can actually draft the email in Gmail, check the calendar in Google, pull the doc from Drive — instead of giving you a generic list of options.

### 07. What you’re working on right now

A short, dated “current state” section. What goals you’re chasing this quarter. Active projects. Open decisions. Things you’ve already tried that didn’t work. **Why it matters:** this is the section that turns your AI from a generic assistant into something closer to a chief of staff. It also keeps the vault alive — if you update this section monthly, the whole document stays fresh.

---

## What it’s not

A Context Vault is not a knowledge base. It is not your wiki. It is not where you dump every doc in your business. The whole point is that it’s short enough to read in a few seconds — that’s why the AI is willing to read it before every answer.

Mine is about eight pages. Most of my clients’ end up between five and twelve.

> Long enough to actually know you. Short enough that an AI will read it every single time.

---

## What you do with it

Once it’s built, you stop opening a blank chat. You start asking your AI things like:

- *“Draft a reply to this lead in my voice.”*
- *“Write three subject lines for an email to my customers about pricing.”*
- *“Look at my calendar this week and tell me where I’m overcommitted.”*
- *“A customer just asked X. What would you say?”*

And what comes back is — for the first time — something that sounds like you wrote it, on your worst day, in five seconds. That’s the bar. That’s the unlock.
